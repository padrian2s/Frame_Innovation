Chapter 1: Challenges — We Are Not Solving Our Problems Anymore

Commentary

Summary

Dorst opens with a powerful diagnosis: we are collectively failing to solve contemporary problems. Boutellier captures the predicament — cacophony, ad hoc policy, fumbling without guiding concepts. The cause: a 'radically new species of problem' that is open, complex, dynamic, and networked.

Open, Complex, Dynamic, and Networked Problems: A new species of problem — so open (unclear boundaries), complex (interconnected elements), dynamic (constantly changing), and networked (mutually influencing) that conventional approaches fail.

Key Patterns

Conventional problem-solving fails not because practitioners are incompetent, but because problems have fundamentally changed. The five syndromes are deeply human patterns that become traps in complex, networked environments.

Practical Applications

Check for the five syndromes in your organization: Lone Warrior? Freeze the World? Self-Made Box? Rational High Ground? Identity around practices? Recognizing these patterns is the first step.

Real-World Example

Consider traffic congestion. Wider roads attract more cars. Copenhagen reframed: instead of managing car traffic, design for bicycles and public life. Result: less congestion AND a better urban experience.
